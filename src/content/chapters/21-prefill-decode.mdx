# Prefill vs Decode: Two Regimes, Two Rooflines

**Where we are.** The [KV-cache](/transformers-systems/chapters/20-kv-cache/) split generation into filling
the cache and extending it. This chapter shows those two operations are not just sequential phases but
*different workloads* — one compute-bound, one memory-bound — measured by different clocks. Confusing them
is the most common inference-performance mistake.

## 1. Prefill: a compute-bound batched pass

<Invariant note="prefill">
**Prefill** processes the entire prompt in one forward pass, computing keys and values for all prompt
tokens at once and filling the cache. Because it operates on many tokens simultaneously, its matrix
multiplications are large and its arithmetic intensity is high: prefill is **compute-bound**, sitting above
the roofline's ridge point. Its latency sets the **time-to-first-token (TTFT)** — how long the user waits
before generation begins.
</Invariant>

## 2. Decode: a memory-bound token stream

<Invariant note="decode">
**Decode** extends the sequence one token at a time. Each step multiplies a *single* token's activation by
the full weight matrices and reads the entire KV-cache — a lot of bytes moved for one token of compute, so
arithmetic intensity is near $1$ and decode is firmly **memory-bound**, below the ridge point. Its per-token
latency is the **time-per-output-token (TPOT)**, and the relevant utilization is model-bandwidth
utilization (MBU), not model-FLOPs utilization.
</Invariant>

The two phases live on opposite sides of the ridge point, so they respond to opposite optimizations:
prefill wants more FLOP/s (larger tiles, better kernels), decode wants more bandwidth and fewer bytes
(a smaller cache, quantization, GQA). An optimization that helps one can be irrelevant to the other.

## 3. Why the distinction is operational

<Invariant note="serve the two phases by their nature">
Because prefill is compute-bound and decode is memory-bound, a server should treat them differently:
batching many *decode* steps together raises the otherwise-wasted arithmetic intensity toward compute-bound
(the motivation for continuous batching), while a long *prefill* can monopolize the device and stall other
requests' decode — which is why chunked prefill interleaves prefill work with ongoing decode. Reporting a
single "tokens/sec" that mixes the two hides which one is the bottleneck; TTFT and TPOT must be reported
separately.
</Invariant>

This is Part 0's methodology again: quote the metric appropriate to the regime. A TPOT quoted as if it were
throughput, or an MFU quoted for a memory-bound decode, misdescribes the system.

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
The lab's serving path times prefill and decode **separately**, exposing the split directly: prefill is a
single batched pass over the prompt (compute-heavy), while decode is a loop of one-token steps whose
per-token cost is dominated by streaming weights and the growing KV-cache — the memory-bound regime the
roofline predicts.
</Renewable>

**Where this goes.** Serving many requests at once means managing many growing KV-caches without wasting
memory. The next chapter borrows an idea from operating systems — paging — to do exactly that.

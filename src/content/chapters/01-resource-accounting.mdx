# Resource Accounting: Parameters, FLOPs, and the Memory Ledger

**Where we are.** The [roofline](/transformers-systems/chapters/00-roofline/) told us *which*
clock bounds a workload. This chapter fixes the quantities that go into those clocks. Before we
run anything, we can predict — to the parameter — how big a model is, how much arithmetic one
training token costs, and how many bytes its training state occupies. These three ledgers are the
"predicted" half of the guide's predict-then-measure loop; every benchmark later in the book is
scored against them.

We work every identity on one model, the reference decoder **R1-tiny** (config frozen in
`reference-model.md` §3 at this book's repo root): $d_\text{model}=256$, $n_\text{layers}=6$, $n_\text{heads}=8$ with
$n_\text{kv}=2$ grouped-query heads of width $d_\text{head}=32$, SwiGLU inner $d_\text{ff}=704$,
vocabulary $V=8192$, context $L=512$, tied embeddings.

## 1. The parameter ledger

<Invariant note="parameter count (architecture-exact)">
A decoder layer's learnable weights are its four attention projections, its feed-forward matrices,
and its norm scales. With grouped-query attention ($n_\text{kv} < n_\text{heads}$) the key/value
projections shrink:
$$P_\text{attn} = \underbrace{d_\text{model}\, n_\text{heads} d_\text{head}}_{W_Q} + \underbrace{2\, d_\text{model}\, n_\text{kv} d_\text{head}}_{W_K,\,W_V} + \underbrace{n_\text{heads} d_\text{head}\, d_\text{model}}_{W_O}.$$
A SwiGLU feed-forward block has three matrices (gate, up, down), so
$P_\text{ffn}=3\,d_\text{model}\,d_\text{ff}$. RMSNorm contributes one scale vector per norm
($2\,d_\text{model}$ per layer). The total is
$P = n_\text{layers}(P_\text{attn}+P_\text{ffn}+P_\text{norm}) + V d_\text{model} + d_\text{model}$,
where the embedding is counted **once** because it is tied to the output projection.
</Invariant>

Instantiated for R1-tiny, every term is small enough to check by hand:

- $W_Q = 256\cdot 256 = 65{,}536$; $W_K=W_V=256\cdot 64 = 16{,}384$; $W_O = 256\cdot 256 = 65{,}536$
  $\Rightarrow P_\text{attn}=163{,}840$. (Note GQA has already halved $K,V$ relative to $8$ full heads.)
- $P_\text{ffn} = 3\cdot 256\cdot 704 = 540{,}672$; $P_\text{norm}=512$.
- Per layer $705{,}024$; six layers give $4{,}230{,}144$; add the final norm ($256$) for
  $4{,}230{,}400$ **non-embedding** parameters — Kaplan's $N$.
- The tied embedding is $V d_\text{model} = 8192\cdot 256 = 2{,}097{,}152$.

$$P_\text{total} = 4{,}230{,}144 + 2{,}097{,}152 + 256 = \mathbf{6{,}327{,}552}.$$

Embedding tying is not a rounding convenience: untied, R1-tiny would carry a *second*
$2.1\text{M}$-parameter output matrix — a third of the model — for no measured quality gain at this
scale. The **embedding-tying** decision is why $2.1\text{M}$ of the ledger, not $4.2\text{M}$, is
vocabulary.

## 2. The FLOP ledger

<Invariant note="the 2× MAC factor and the 6·N rule">
A multiply–accumulate is two floating-point operations — one multiply, one add — so a matmul with
$N$ weight-multiplies costs $2N$ FLOPs of forward compute per token. The backward pass computes
gradients with respect to *both* activations and weights, costing about twice the forward, so total
training compute is $\approx 3\times$ forward:
$$C_\text{train} \approx 6\, N_\text{matmul}\ \text{FLOPs/token}.$$
This is the "$6ND$" rule. It counts only matmul parameters — norms are elementwise and negligible,
and, crucially, it **omits the attention score/context term**, which scales with context length and
must be added separately.
</Invariant>

R1-tiny has $N_\text{matmul}=6{,}324{,}224$ (the per-layer attention + FFN weights plus the tied
embedding matmul). This is **not** Kaplan's non-embedding $N=4{,}230{,}400$ from §1: Kaplan's rule
excludes the embedding, while our convention deliberately includes the tied-embedding matmul — at
this scale it is a third of all matmul work. Two $N$'s share the symbol but not the value, and it is
$N_\text{matmul}$ that the numbers contract hashes. So the dense term is
$6N_\text{matmul}=37{,}945{,}344$ FLOPs/token. The attention
score ($QK^\top$) and context ($A V$) products add, per token,
$n_\text{layers}\cdot 2\cdot(2 L\, n_\text{heads}d_\text{head})$ forward, tripled for training:
$3\cdot 6\cdot 2\cdot(2\cdot 512\cdot 256) = 9{,}437{,}184$ FLOPs/token. Total:

$$C_\text{train} = 37{,}945{,}344 + 9{,}437{,}184 = \mathbf{47{,}382{,}528}\ \text{FLOPs/token}.$$

At the training batch of $32\times 512 = 16{,}384$ tokens, one optimizer step is
$\approx 776\ \text{GFLOP}$. The attention term is $20\%$ of the total here **because** $L=512$ is
short; at $L=8192$ it would dominate — the reason the $6ND$ rule alone silently under-counts
long-context training.

## 3. The memory ledger

<Invariant note="training-state bytes per parameter">
AdamW training state is a fixed set of per-parameter tensors — the weight, its gradient, and Adam's
first and second moments — so bytes/parameter is set by the precision recipe alone, independent of
model size. Under the pilot's **pure-fp32** recipe each of the four costs 4 B:
$$M_\text{train-state} = (4+4+4+4)\, P_\text{total} = 16\, P_\text{total}\ \text{bytes}.$$
The **optimizer state** proper — the two Adam moments — is $8$ of those $16$ bytes: as large as
weights and gradients combined.
</Invariant>

For R1-tiny, $16\cdot 6{,}327{,}552 = 101{,}240{,}832$ bytes $\approx 101.2\ \text{MB}$ of **train
state** — the *fixed* cost, before a single activation — of which the two moments are
$50.6\ \text{MB}$.

The frozen contract (§3) targets a different recipe, **mixed precision**: bf16 weight (2 B) + bf16
gradient (2 B) + fp32 master copy (4 B) + moments (4 B + 4 B) = 16 bytes/parameter — and 18 with
fp32 gradients. That recipe is design-forward, not measured: the pilot deviations register
(ADR 0002) records that the run below is pure fp32. That both recipes total "16 B/param" is a
coincidence of two different unit conventions — exactly the same-scalar-different-units trap the
numbers contract warns about — so a bytes-per-parameter figure means nothing until its
decomposition is named.

Activations are the variable cost: they scale with batch $\times$ sequence and are what
activation-recomputation trades compute for. Here a hand estimate misleads: counting only
$d_\text{model}$-wide tensors gives $\approx 403\ \text{MB}$ at the contract's $32\times 512$ token
batch, yet that formula omits the fp32 logits tensor alone
($16{,}384\times 8{,}192 \approx 537\ \text{MB}$ at batch 32 — larger than the whole estimate) and
the $d_\text{ff}$-wide SwiGLU intermediates. The measurement is the honest anchor: the run below
peaks at $1{,}171{,}365{,}888$ bytes $\approx 1.17\ \text{GB}$ at batch $8$ — one *quarter* of
those tokens — of which $\approx 1.07\ \text{GB}$ beyond the train state is activations, workspace,
and allocator overhead. It is the measured peak, not the estimate, that says a
$6.3\text{M}$-parameter model fits comfortably on an $8\ \text{GB}$ card, with the envelope scaled
to the full contract batch still well inside the budget.

The numbers above are *predicted* by a pure-stdlib fixture. Because they are contracted, the training
run measures them and must agree:

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
Training R1-tiny on real R0 tokens under the pure-fp32 recipe, the harness measures the train state
directly (summing `weight + grad + exp_avg + exp_avg_sq` over every parameter group) at
**101.2 MB**, matching the predicted $101{,}240{,}832$ bytes, counts exactly $6{,}327{,}552$
parameters, and records a peak allocation of $1{,}171{,}365{,}888$ bytes (≈**1.17 GB**) at batch 8
— the ledger and the allocator agree to the byte.
</Renewable>

Reproduce the predicted side yourself; it needs no GPU and no framework:

**Where this goes.** These three ledgers are inputs to every downstream decision: the FLOP ledger
plus a compute budget fixes model *shape* (the scaling-laws chapter); the memory ledger fixes what
fits per device and therefore the parallelism plan; and both are frozen into the **numbers contract**
so a chapter that quotes "6.3M params" and a chapter that quotes a throughput can never rest on
different arithmetic.

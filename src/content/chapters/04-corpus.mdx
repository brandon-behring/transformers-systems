# The Corpus: Acquisition, Licensing, and an Immutable Provenance

**Where we are.** Part 0 gave us the discipline for numbers. Part 1 builds the thing those numbers
describe: **R0**, the data-and-interface contract every later model consumes. It begins with the
corpus — and the first lesson of production data work is that a corpus is not a pile of text you
happen to have, but a *contract* you can restate, license, and re-derive byte-for-byte.

## 1. A corpus is a contract

<Invariant note="the five clauses of a corpus contract">
A reproducible corpus fixes: (i) a **source** with a pinned revision (which dataset, which split,
how many documents); (ii) a **license** that permits your use and redistribution of derived
artifacts; (iii) a **normalization** — the exact text transformation applied, stated, not assumed;
(iv) **splits** with no leakage between train and eval; and (v) an **immutable identifier** — a
checksum over the assembled bytes — so "the corpus" names one specific artifact forever. Drop any
clause and "trained on FineWeb" stops being a claim anyone can check.
</Invariant>

The license clause is the one engineers most often skip and lawyers most reliably catch. R0 draws
from **FineWeb-Edu** (the education-filtered subset of FineWeb), released under **ODC-By-1.0** — an
attribution license that permits derived works. Recording the license *in the manifest*, next to the
bytes, means a downstream user of R1 or R3 inherits the provenance rather than having to reconstruct
it.

## 2. Normalization is a decision, not a default

R0's normalization is deliberately minimal and explicitly stated: strip surrounding whitespace,
replace intra-document newlines with spaces (so one line is one document), and rely on UTF-8 to carry
an implicit NFC form. Minimal is a choice with consequences — aggressive normalization (lowercasing,
Unicode folding, punctuation stripping) discards signal a byte-level tokenizer could have used, while
*no* normalization lets encoding noise masquerade as vocabulary. The point is not which policy is
best but that the policy is **written down and checksummed**, so two runs of the pipeline cannot
silently disagree on what "the text" was.

Deduplication belongs to this clause too: near-duplicate documents inflate apparent data and let a
model memorize instead of generalize. FineWeb-Edu is deduplicated upstream, so R0 inherits it; a
corpus built from raw crawl would have to dedup explicitly and record that it did.

## 3. Immutability by checksum

R0's build script assembles the corpus, then hashes it — and hashes the tokenizer and the batches
derived from it — into `r0/manifest.yml`. The manifest is the corpus's identity: change one document
and the SHA-256 changes, and every artifact pinned to the old hash is now provably stale. This is the
[numbers contract](/transformers-systems/chapters/03-numbers-contract/) applied to data instead of to
FLOPs.

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
R0 assembles **8,000 FineWeb-Edu documents** (config `sample-10BT`, ODC-By-1.0) and records
`corpus_sha256`, the byte count, the normalization string, and the license in `r0/manifest.yml`. If
FineWeb-Edu is unreachable the script falls back to a pinned public-domain text and records *that* in
the manifest — the pipeline never silently substitutes data without leaving a trace.
</Renewable>

<Versioned as_of="2026-07-17" version="torch==2.13.0">
```bash
python r0/build_r0.py
# [r0] corpus docs=8000 (HuggingFaceFW/fineweb-edu)  vocab=8192  batches=(256, 512)
# [r0] shas: corpus=… tok=… batches=…   → written to r0/manifest.yml
```
</Versioned>

**Where this goes.** The corpus is the raw material; the next chapter turns it into tokens with a
byte-level BPE tokenizer whose vocabulary we can rebuild and whose special tokens reserve room for the
document-AI interface.

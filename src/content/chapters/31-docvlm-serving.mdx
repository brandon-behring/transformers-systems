# Serving the Doc-VLM: Grounding, Constrained Decoding, and the Deliverables Matrix

**Where we are.** The [trained doc-VLM](/transformers-systems/chapters/30-docvlm-training/) turns a page into
markup. This final chapter serves it — with the serving stack of Part 5, plus three document-specific
guarantees — and then closes the guide by showing that R3 is not a new system but the whole book's machinery,
reused.

## 1. Constrained decoding makes malformed output impossible

<Invariant note="a grammar over the output vocabulary">
Structured markup has a grammar — tags open and close, tables nest rows and cells — and free-form decoding
can violate it, producing an unparseable answer. **Constrained decoding** enforces the grammar at generation
time: at each step it masks the logits to only the tokens the grammar permits next, so the output is
*guaranteed* well-formed by construction. This turns "the model usually produces valid markup" into "the
model cannot produce invalid markup" — a correctness guarantee moved from hope to mechanism, at the cost of a
grammar-mask computation per token.
</Invariant>

Constrained decoding composes with the paged KV-cache and continuous batching of Part 5 unchanged; it is an
extra mask on the decode step's logits, not a new serving architecture.

## 2. Grounding: tie every claim to a region

<Invariant note="evidence localization">
A document answer that cannot be traced to *where* on the page it came from is unverifiable. **Grounding**
(evidence localization) makes the model emit, alongside content, the region it derived that content from — a
box or a coordinate token sequence. This is the coordinate-token interface from Part 1 put to work: geometry
is emitted as tokens, so a grounded answer is one token stream carrying both the *what* and the *where*.
Grounding is what lets the eval-as-CI metric check not just that an answer is right but that it is
*supported* — the difference between an OCR system and a citable one.
</Invariant>

## 3. High-resolution tiling for dense pages

<Invariant note="tiling trades tokens for legibility">
The fixed visual-token budget that keeps decode cheap also caps resolution — and a dense page (fine print, a
large table) can exceed what one tile at that budget resolves. **Tiling** splits the page into overlapping
crops, encodes each within the budget, and concatenates their visual tokens, trading a longer visual prefix
(more KV-cache, higher TTFT) for legibility of small glyphs. This is the same prefill/decode economics of
Part 5: tiling lengthens the compute-bound prefill to protect the accuracy of the memory-bound decode.
</Invariant>

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
The pilot serves the doc-VLM through the same generation path as R1, proving the interface end to end;
constrained decoding (a grammar over the document vocabulary), evidence localization (coordinate tokens), and
high-resolution tiling are specified as the production serving profile — a Q(·) projection over R3 — and are
the scale-tier build. Each is a documented extension of a Part-5 mechanism, not a new stack.
</Renewable>

## 4. The deliverables matrix: one reference model, carried through

<Invariant note="what the guide delivered">
R3 is the proof of the guide's thesis: a document-AI system is not a separate discipline but the text spine
reused. It shares R0's tokenizer, R1's decoder and architecture, Part 3's training loop and kernels, Part 4's
sharding, Part 5's serving and paged cache, and Part 6's alignment and eval-as-CI — adding only an encoder, a
connector, and a grammar. Every number traces to a runnable fixture; every claim to a strict-live source; and
one reference-model family runs unbroken from a parameter count to a served, grounded document parse.
</Invariant>

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
The full arc holds on one 8 GB card: R1-tiny (6,327,552 params, predicted and measured to the byte) trains on
R0 tokens, computes exact attention through a Triton kernel, resumes bit-for-bit, reshards across world size,
serves through a paged KV-cache with output parity, and — as R3 — turns a page image into exact document
markup. What exceeds 8 GB (7B decoders, multi-GPU throughput, real-corpus training) is named as the scale
tier, designed-for and not overclaimed.
</Renewable>

**Where this ends.** This is the last chapter. The guide set out to carry one reference model from *decide*
to *serve*, nuts and bolts, with every number honest — and R3 closes that arc: the document-AI north star is
the same model, the same systems, and the same numbers contract, pointed at pixels. The measurement discipline
of Part 0 is the through-line; the reference model is the object; and the scale tier is the horizon the whole
guide was built to reach.

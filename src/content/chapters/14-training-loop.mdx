# The Training Loop: Optimizer, Mixed Precision, and Checkpoints

**Where we are.** We have a correct forward pass and a parity-pinned attention kernel. This chapter wraps
the model in the loop that actually learns: compute a loss, backpropagate, take an optimizer step, and —
crucially — save a checkpoint that captures enough to *resume*. Every choice here is one the [numbers
contract](/transformers-systems/chapters/03-numbers-contract/) and the [memory
ledger](/transformers-systems/chapters/01-resource-accounting/) already priced.

## 1. AdamW: decoupled weight decay

<Invariant note="AdamW">
AdamW maintains, per parameter, a first moment (a smoothed gradient) and a second moment (a smoothed
squared gradient), and steps in the direction of the first scaled by the inverse root of the second.
Its defining choice is that weight decay is **decoupled** — applied directly to the weights rather than
folded into the gradient — so regularization does not interact with the adaptive scaling. The two
moments *are* the optimizer state: $8$ of the $16$ bytes-per-parameter in the memory ledger's fp32
train state, with the weights ($4$) and gradients ($4$) accounting for the rest.
</Invariant>

R1-tiny uses the standard large-model settings: learning rate $3\times 10^{-4}$, $\beta=(0.9, 0.95)$,
weight decay $0.1$, and gradient clipping at norm $1.0$. At $\beta_2=0.95$ the second moment averages
over a materially shorter horizon than the $0.999$ default — roughly $20$ steps versus $1{,}000$. The
contract's WSD learning-rate schedule is design-forward: the pilot runs at constant LR, and no scheduler
exists in the lab (see the pilot deviations register, ADR 0002). These settings are not tuned here —
they are the reference recipe, frozen so a later chapter's numbers do not silently depend on an
untracked hyperparameter.

## 2. Mixed precision: compute low, accumulate high

<Invariant note="the master-weight policy">
Mixed-precision training computes the forward and backward in a low-precision format (bf16) for speed and
memory, while keeping an **fp32 master copy** of the weights and doing the optimizer update and the
gradient reductions in fp32. The reason is numerical: a bf16 weight cannot represent the tiny updates a
late-training step applies, so accumulating those updates in fp32 preserves them. Compute low, accumulate
high.
</Invariant>

This is the *design policy* — the recipe the frozen contract (§3) targets at the scale tier — not the
pilot's measured configuration: the pilot run is pure fp32 AdamW, with no autocast and no master copy
(see the pilot deviations register, ADR 0002). The ledgers differ accordingly: the pilot's fp32 ledger is
$4{+}4{+}4{+}4 = 16$ B/param, while the contract recipe's honest ledger is a bf16 weight ($2$) + a bf16
gradient ($2$) + an fp32 master ($4$) + two moments ($4{+}4$), for a total of $16$ B/param — or $18$
with fp32 gradients — so the two totals collide at $16$ by coincidence. bf16's wider exponent (versus
fp16) also removes the loss-scaling machinery fp16 needs, simplifying the loop.

## 3. A checkpoint is the whole state, not the weights

<Invariant note="what a resumable checkpoint contains">
A checkpoint that stores only the model weights cannot resume training — it has thrown away the
optimizer moments (so the first resumed step takes a wrong-sized stride) and the data cursor (so it
re-sees or skips data). A *resumable* checkpoint captures the full training state: model weights,
optimizer moments, the dataloader cursor, and every random-number generator stream — plus, when a
scheduler exists, its position (the pilot's loop is constant-LR and has none). The weights are a
minority of the checkpoint's bytes: the two moments alone are twice their size.
</Invariant>

This is the setup for the next chapter's gate: the checkpoint is only correct if resuming from it
reproduces an uninterrupted run, and that is only possible if the checkpoint is complete.

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
R1-tiny trains with pure fp32 AdamW (lr $3\times10^{-4}$, $\beta=(0.9,0.95)$, wd $0.1$, grad-clip $1.0$;
no autocast, no master copy) on real R0 tokens: loss falls from $9.07$ at the first warmup step to a
trailing-10-step mean of $6.26$, and the directly-measured train state ($101.2$ MB —
weights + grads + moments) matches the ledger's prediction to the byte. The contract's bf16-compute +
fp32-master recipe is the scale-tier target, not what ran (see the pilot deviations register, ADR 0002).
</Renewable>

<Versioned as_of="2026-07-17" version="torch==2.13.0">
```python
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(m.parameters(), lr=3e-4, betas=(0.9, 0.95), weight_decay=0.1)
_, loss = m(x, y)                 # forward (pure fp32 — no autocast)
opt.zero_grad(); loss.backward()  # backward (fp32 grads)
torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(m.parameters(), 1.0)
opt.step()                        # fp32 update — the moments are 8 of the 16 B/param train state
```
</Versioned>

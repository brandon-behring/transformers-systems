# Scaling Laws as a Design Instrument

**Where we are.** The [family DAG](/transformers-systems/chapters/08-model-family/) fixed *which*
models exist. This chapter is the instrument that sizes them. We use scaling laws not to forecast a
benchmark but as an engineering tool: given a fixed compute budget, how many parameters and how many
tokens minimize loss? The formal companion guide proves the allocation theorem; here we treat it as a
design dial and read off what it says about the reference model. *(For the theorem's proof and its
exact regularity conditions, see the formal guide's scaling-laws chapter.)*

## 1. The loss surface has a separable shape

<Invariant note="the separable scaling-law fit">
Across a wide regime, pretraining loss as a function of parameters $N$ and training tokens $D$ fits a
separable power law
$$L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}},$$
where $E$ is the irreducible loss and the two additive terms are the finite-model and finite-data
penalties. This is an *empirical fit*, not a law of nature: the coefficients depend on the tokenizer,
the data mixture, the architecture, and the recipe, and it describes pretraining loss in a stated
regime — not downstream-task behavior.
</Invariant>

The value of the fit is that it is *separable*: the parameter penalty and the data penalty add, so a
compute budget spent on one trades against the other along a clean contour.

## 2. From a budget to a shape

Training compute is, to the dense-FLOP proxy of Part 0, $C \approx 6ND$. Minimizing $L$ on the contour
$6ND = C$ gives the compute-optimal allocation:

<Invariant note="compute-optimal allocation">
$$N_\text{opt} \propto C^{\,\beta/(\alpha+\beta)}, \qquad D_\text{opt} \propto C^{\,\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)}.$$
Parameters and tokens each grow as a power of the budget, with exponents set by the *ratio* of the
fitted decay rates. When the two rates are equal ($\alpha=\beta$) both exponents collapse to $1/2$:
optimal $N$ and $D$ each scale as $\sqrt{C}$, and the optimal token-to-parameter ratio is constant.
Chinchilla's fit put that ratio near **20 tokens per parameter** — but the ratio is constant in $C$
*only* under the equal-exponent symmetry; in general it drifts with the budget.
</Invariant>

This is the "$6ND$" rule's payoff: it converts a dollar figure (GPU-hours) into a target $(N, D)$
before a single step runs. It is also where the proxy's honesty matters — $6ND$ is a *dense-training
FLOP proxy*, not wall-clock; utilization, memory traffic, and the attention term (Part 0 §2) still need
separate accounting when the budget is a real cluster and a real clock.

## 3. When you should *not* be compute-optimal

<Invariant note="the training-only optimum is not the lifecycle optimum">
Compute-optimal allocation minimizes *training* loss per training FLOP. It ignores inference. A model
that will serve many tokens has a lifecycle cost dominated by *serving*, and there a smaller model
trained on more tokens than Chinchilla prescribes — deliberately "over-trained" — can be rational:
you pay more training compute once to buy a cheaper model forever. Inference-aware scaling makes this
trade explicit. Over-training here means *more tokens at fixed model size than the training-only rule
selects*, not training past convergence.
</Invariant>

R1-tiny departs from compute-optimal for a blunter reason, and naming it is the point of this chapter:
its shape is set by a **hardware constraint**, not a scaling optimum. At $6.3\text{M}$ parameters it is
far smaller than any compute-optimal target, because the binding constraint is that its training state
plus activations must fit an 8 GB card (the [memory ledger](/transformers-systems/chapters/01-resource-accounting/)).
Scaling laws are still the instrument — they tell us *exactly how far* R1-tiny sits from optimal, and in
which direction a production sibling would move.

<Renewable last_measured="2026-07-17" measured_on="Tier-0 RTX 2070 SUPER" source="https://github.com/brandon-behring/transformers-systems-lab">
R1-tiny is a deliberately **under-parameterized pilot**: sized by the Tier-0 memory budget, not by a
compute-optimal fit. The training run's loss descends monotonically ($9.07 \to 6.26$) — the model is
learning, but it is nowhere near the data-saturated regime a compute-optimal model targets, exactly as
the ledger predicts for its size.
</Renewable>

**Where this goes.** Scaling laws fixed the *size*; the next chapter fixes the *shape at the layer
level* — the specific normalization, positional encoding, activation, and attention grouping that make
R1 a modern decoder rather than a 2017 one.
